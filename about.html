The long version

How I got here.

01 —

Claremont, originally

I grew up in Claremont. Went to school here, worked a few high school jobs here, left for college, came back for holidays. The kind of hometown that you don't appreciate until you've spent a few years somewhere else.

I got my nursing degree and, like a lot of people in my twenties, needed to go somewhere new. Portland made sense — beautiful, weird, had a real aesthetic medicine scene. I ended up staying ten years.

02 —

Ten years in Portland

I started out doing traditional nursing, then moved into aesthetics in my second year. It clicked immediately. I loved the precision, the artistry, the way a few small changes could help someone feel more like themselves.

I apprenticed under injectors I still look up to. Worked in practices where I saw every kind of patient — from first-timers who were terrified of needles to clients who'd been getting work done for thirty years. Learned what good aesthetic work looks like, and — more importantly — learned what overdone looks like.

I'd rather under-treat and have you come back, than chase a trend and have you regret it.

Somewhere around year six, I started teaching. Other injectors would come to watch me work, and it turned into something I did regularly — small group trainings, one-on-one mentorships. To date, I've trained over 500 aesthetic professionals. It sharpened me more than anything else I've done.

03 —

Coming home

I met my husband Vince in Portland. We had our son James. And somewhere in the middle of all of that, I started thinking about home.

Claremont has this really lovely thing where almost nothing has changed since I grew up. The same trees. The same streets. But also — it doesn't really have what I wanted to build. A medspa run by a nurse, not an algorithm. Small, considered, personal.

So I opened Boon. Named after something simple — a boon, meaning a gift or a favor — because that's what I want your time in my chair to feel like.

04 —

Now

Boon is small, on purpose. I do every consultation, every treatment. I want to know your face, your life, what you're going through. I want to tell you no sometimes. And I want you to leave looking like yourself — just better-rested, a little more at ease in the mirror.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

— Jessie

How I practice

Three rules I won't break.

These aren't marketing principles. They're the ones I learned the hard way over ten years, and the ones I teach other injectors.

01

Consult first, always

No injection on the first visit — not ever. We talk, I look at your face in natural light, we make a plan. You go home, think about it, decide if you want to come back.

02

Start small

You can always add more. You can't take it out. I'd rather see you three times over a year than try to do everything in one session and push too far.

03

Honest answers

If what you want isn't realistic, or if I think you'd regret it, I'll say so. Sometimes the answer is "not right now." Sometimes it's "that's a surgeon, not me."

Off the clock

A life outside the chair.

I live in Claremont with my husband Vince and our son James. Outside of work, I'm usually hiking around the Wilderness Park, at one of the coffee shops in the Village, or pretending I'm going to take up pottery again.

I mention this because I think it matters who's holding the needle. You're not going to love your injector if they treat this as a production line. I treat ten to twelve people a day, max. Usually fewer. Always on purpose.
